Seacoast Communities

Find the town that fits your life.

Each Seacoast town has its own pace, housing patterns, coastline access, commute considerations, and personality. I wrote these pages in my own voice to help you compare them in a more useful way.

a walkable harbor city with historic streets, restaurants, arts, and waterfront energy

Portsmouth

Portsmouth is often the first place people imagine when they picture Seacoast living. When I help clients think through Portsmouth, I focus on fit: whether they want the pace of downtown, an older home with character, a condominium near restaurants, or quieter residential pockets with quick access to the water and commuter routes. Portsmouth can feel exciting, convenient, and deeply local all at once, but the right property depends on how you want daily life to feel.

Read about Portsmouth
coastal homes, beaches, quiet neighborhoods, and a strong sense of place

Rye

Rye is close to my heart because it captures so much of what people love about the New Hampshire Seacoast: coastline, open space, neighborhood streets, and a quieter residential rhythm. When I work with buyers in Rye, we talk carefully about beach proximity, flood considerations, renovation expectations, and how each part of town lives differently. For sellers, presentation and pricing matter because Rye buyers are often comparing lifestyle as much as square footage.

Read about Rye
beach access, year-round neighborhoods, and a broad range of coastal property types

Hampton

Hampton is more nuanced than many people realize. There is the energy of Hampton Beach, but there are also established neighborhoods, commuter-friendly locations, marsh views, and homes that appeal to both year-round residents and seasonal buyers. I like helping clients separate the vacation impression of Hampton from the practical realities of owning there, including parking, rental potential, maintenance, and what different locations mean for resale.

Read about Hampton
historic downtown character, schools, commuting options, and a classic New England feel

Exeter

Exeter appeals to clients who want history, a true downtown, and access to both the Seacoast and inland New Hampshire. I tend to frame Exeter around lifestyle: do you want to be close to shops and restaurants, near trails and parks, or tucked into a neighborhood with more space? It can be a strong fit for buyers who want a classic town center without being directly on the coast.

Read about Exeter
a growing Seacoast city with downtown energy, mill history, and relative value

Dover

Dover is one of the most interesting Seacoast markets because it gives many buyers more room to work while still offering a real downtown, restaurants, parks, and access to Portsmouth, Durham, and southern Maine. For sellers, Dover’s variety means positioning is important. A condo, an antique home, a newer subdivision property, and a waterfront-adjacent home all need to be presented differently.

Read about Dover
a college-town setting with trails, community life, and access to Great Bay

Durham

Durham has a distinctive feel because of the University of New Hampshire, but it is not only a student town. It offers established neighborhoods, access to trails and conservation land, and a quieter pace around Great Bay. When I help clients consider Durham, we talk about timing, rental influence, school-year rhythms, and whether they want the energy of a university community or a more tucked-away residential setting.

Read about Durham
space, farms, commuter convenience, and access to both Portsmouth and Exeter

Stratham

Stratham is a strong option for buyers who want Seacoast access without feeling coastal in the day-to-day sense. It has a more residential, spread-out feel, with larger lots in many areas, local farms, and quick access to Portsmouth, Exeter, and major routes. I often recommend Stratham to clients who want a balance of privacy, convenience, and long-term livability.

Read about Stratham
a small coastal town with residential privacy and limited shoreline

North Hampton

North Hampton is one of those towns where small differences in location matter. It offers a quieter coastal feel, beautiful residential areas, and a limited stretch of New Hampshire coastline. When clients consider North Hampton, I help them compare proximity to the beach, access to Route 1, lot setting, and whether the home feels private while still being connected to Portsmouth, Rye, and Hampton.

Read about North Hampton
a small-town setting close to Portsmouth, Great Bay, and commuter routes

Greenland

Greenland is often a smart fit for people who want to be near Portsmouth but prefer a quieter residential setting. It has a strong community feel, access to Great Bay surroundings, and convenient routes in multiple directions. For buyers, the key is understanding inventory, because Greenland is smaller and good options can be limited. For sellers, that scarcity can be an advantage when the home is prepared and priced well.

Read about Greenland
island living at the mouth of Portsmouth Harbor with rare coastal inventory

New Castle

New Castle is a very particular market because the setting is so rare. It offers island living, harbor views, historic character, and immediate access to Portsmouth, but inventory is naturally limited. When I talk with clients about New Castle, I focus on patience, property condition, exposure, and long-term goals. This is not a town where every opportunity can be measured only by price per square foot.

Read about New Castle
historic homes, farms, rural character, and proximity to the coast

Hampton Falls

Hampton Falls can be a wonderful choice for buyers who want a quieter, more rural feel while staying close to the beach, Exeter, and commuter routes. I think of it as a town where land, setting, and history matter. For sellers, the story of the property can be especially important: acreage, gardens, outbuildings, updates, and how the home supports a slower Seacoast lifestyle.

Read about Hampton Falls
beach access, commuter convenience, and southern Seacoast value

Seabrook

Seabrook sits at an important edge of the New Hampshire Seacoast, with beach access, proximity to Massachusetts, and a mix of year-round and seasonal considerations. It can be a practical market for buyers who want coastal access while watching value carefully. I help clients look closely at location, flood exposure, rental considerations, and how different parts of town support different goals.
